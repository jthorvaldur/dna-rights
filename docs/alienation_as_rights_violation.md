# Alienation as Rights Violation

When one parent severs the other parent's natural bond to their offspring, it is not merely a psychological phenomenon or a custody issue. It is a violation of a natural right.

## Beyond Psychology

Parental alienation is typically framed as a psychological or behavioral problem — one parent "turning the children against" the other. This framing is inadequate because it:

- Treats alienation as a custody management issue rather than a rights violation
- Gives the court discretion to weigh alienation against other "best interest" factors
- Allows the alienating parent to argue that the offspring's "wishes" justify the separation
- Keeps the entire dispute within the court's legal framework

The natural rights framing is different: alienation is the unauthorized severance of a biological bond. No human being has the authority to sever another person's bond to their biological offspring. Not a judge. Not the other parent. Not the state.

## The Jurisdictional Violation

The alienating parent acts as if they possess an authority that does not exist:

1. **The alienating parent did not create the other parent's biological bond.** They cannot destroy what they did not create.
2. **No court granted the alienating parent authority to sever a natural bond.** Even a custody order does not authorize alienation — it allocates legal parenting time.
3. **The biological bond persists regardless of alienation.** DNA does not change because one parent poisons the offspring's perception. The offspring remain the offspring of both biological parents.

Alienation is, in jurisdictional terms, an act without authority — an individual exercising a power that no human authority possesses: the power to sever a biological bond.

## Framing Alienation as a Tort

Instead of (or in addition to) raising alienation as a custody issue, frame it as a tort — a civil wrong:

### Interference with Natural Rights
- **The right:** The biological bond between parent and offspring (natural right, recognized in *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000))
- **The interference:** Deliberate actions by the other parent to prevent, undermine, or destroy the bond
- **The harm:** Loss of the natural parent-offspring relationship, which cannot be fully restored by any court order
- **The remedy:** Damages (compensatory and punitive) and injunctive relief (restoration of access)

### Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress
- Alienating conduct — systematic manipulation of offspring to reject a biological parent — is extreme and outrageous
- The alienating parent knows (or should know) that severing a parent-offspring bond causes severe emotional distress
- The harm is to both the alienated parent and the offspring

### Fraud
- When an alienating parent makes false statements about the other parent to the offspring, to the court, or to third parties, it may constitute fraud
- False claims of abuse or neglect used to justify separation are particularly actionable

## International Law

International frameworks explicitly protect against the separation of parents from offspring:

### UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)
- **Article 9(1):** "States Parties shall ensure that a child shall not be separated from his or her parents against their will, except when competent authorities subject to judicial review determine, in accordance with applicable law and procedures, that such separation is necessary for the best interests of the child."
- **Article 9(3):** "States Parties shall respect the right of the child who is separated from one or both parents to maintain personal relations and direct contact with both parents on a regular basis, except if it is contrary to the child's best interests."

Note: Even the CRC, which uses "child" (a legal term), acknowledges that separation requires *necessity* determined by *competent authorities* — not the unilateral action of one parent.

### European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)
- **Article 8:** "Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life." Alienation violates this right for both the targeted parent and the offspring.

### Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)
- **Article 16(3):** "The family is the natural and fundamental group unit of society and is entitled to protection by society and the State." Alienation is the destruction of the family unit — the opposite of the protection required by the UDHR.

## The Scale of the Violation

Consider what alienation actually does:

1. **Severs a biological bond** that exists by nature and was established at conception
2. **Deprives the offspring** of their natural right to know and be raised by both biological parents
3. **Deprives the targeted parent** of their natural right to their offspring
4. **Substitutes one parent's will** for the natural order — one human being decides that another human being shall not have access to their own biological offspring
5. **Uses the offspring as instruments** — the offspring themselves are weaponized against their own biological parent

No court order authorizes this. No statute permits it. No natural law supports it. It is a violation of the most fundamental natural bond that exists between human beings.

## Practical Assertion

When raising alienation in a legal proceeding, frame it in natural rights terms:

> "The opposing party has engaged in a systematic campaign to sever the biological bond between Respondent and his/her offspring. This bond exists by nature, is established by DNA, and is beyond the jurisdiction of any human authority to destroy.
>
> No court order authorized the opposing party to alienate Respondent's offspring. No statute grants any individual the authority to sever a biological parent-offspring bond. The opposing party's conduct constitutes interference with a natural right — the most fundamental of all human relationships.
>
> Respondent demands that this Court: (1) recognize the natural right to the parent-offspring bond; (2) find that the opposing party's alienating conduct violates that right; and (3) order restoration of the biological bond through immediate, unrestricted access to Respondent's offspring."
