# DNA as Claim

DNA establishes standing independent of court orders. It is not evidence submitted to a court for evaluation — it is the claim itself.

## DNA Is a Biological Fact

A DNA test does not grant parentage. It reveals what already exists. The biological relationship between parent and offspring was established at conception. The DNA test merely makes the existing fact visible.

This distinction matters:
- **Legal paternity** is determined by a court. It can be established, modified, or terminated by judicial order.
- **Biological parentage** is determined by DNA. It cannot be established, modified, or terminated by any human authority.

A court can declare that a man is the legal father of a child. A court can declare that a man is *not* the legal father of a child. But no court can alter the biological fact of parentage. The DNA is what it is.

## "The Living Soul of My DNA"

Biological identity is not a legal argument — it is a jurisdictional fact. When a parent says "these are the offspring of my DNA," they are not making a legal claim that requires court adjudication. They are stating a biological fact that exists independent of any legal proceeding.

The court can adjudicate legal relationships. It cannot adjudicate biological identity. DNA is not within the court's jurisdiction because it was not created by any legal process.

## Legal Paternity vs. Biological Parentage

| Dimension | Legal Paternity | Biological Parentage |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Established by | Court order, marriage presumption, voluntary acknowledgment | Conception (DNA) |
| Modified by | Court order | Nothing — it is immutable |
| Terminated by | Court order (termination of parental rights) | Nothing — it is permanent |
| Evidence | Marriage certificate, court records, acknowledgment form | DNA test |
| Jurisdiction | State family courts | Natural law (no court has jurisdiction) |
| Effect of court order | Can change legal status | Cannot change biological fact |

## DNA as Evidence of Standing

In legal proceedings, "standing" means the right to bring a claim. Normally, standing is determined by statute or case law. But biological parentage provides a form of standing that supersedes statutory requirements:

- A biological parent has standing to assert their natural right to their offspring regardless of what any statute says
- DNA is not evidence *of* standing — it *is* standing
- A court that refuses to recognize DNA-established standing is refusing to acknowledge a biological fact

## Connection to Land

"Of your flesh" parallels "of your land." The structure of the claim is identical:

- **Land claim at common law:** This is my land. I hold it by right. My title does not derive from any government grant. (Allodial title)
- **Offspring claim by natural right:** These are my offspring. They are of my DNA. My parentage does not derive from any court order.

In both cases, the claim precedes the legal system. The legal system may *recognize* the claim, but it did not *create* the claim. And what the legal system did not create, it cannot destroy.

The corporate legal fiction separates the man from his property through layers of legal abstraction (deeds, titles, liens, encumbrances). The same fiction separates the parent from their offspring through layers of legal abstraction (custody orders, visitation schedules, parenting plans, guardian ad litem reports).

Strip away the abstraction and the biological fact remains: these offspring carry this parent's DNA.

## Sending In the DNA Claim

How to frame a DNA-based assertion in court filings:

### 1. Establish the Biological Fact
> "Respondent is the biological father/mother of [offspring names], as established by DNA. This biological relationship was created at conception and exists independent of any legal proceeding."

### 2. Assert the Natural Right
> "The parent-offspring bond is a natural right recognized by the Supreme Court as 'the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests.' (*Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65 (2000)). This right is not created by statute and cannot be extinguished by court order."

### 3. Challenge the Court's Jurisdiction Over the Biological Bond
> "This Court has jurisdiction over legal relationships created by statute. The biological bond between a parent and their offspring was not created by statute. Respondent respectfully requests that this Court identify the specific authority under which it claims jurisdiction over a biological fact."

### 4. Demand Performance
> "If this Court asserts jurisdiction over the biological parent-offspring bond, Respondent demands that the Court specifically identify: (a) the source of that authority, (b) the legal basis for overriding a natural right, and (c) the standard by which a biological fact becomes subject to judicial modification."

## Key Principle

DNA does not ask for the court's permission to exist. The parent-offspring bond does not require judicial ratification. The claim is the biology. The biology is the claim.
