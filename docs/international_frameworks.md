# International Frameworks

International law on family rights — how global legal instruments support the natural rights argument.

## UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)

The CRC is the most widely ratified international human rights treaty. The United States has signed but not ratified it — which means it is persuasive authority but not binding law in U.S. courts.

### Key Articles

**Article 7 — Right to Know Parents**
> "The child shall be registered immediately after birth and shall have the right from birth to a name, the right to acquire a nationality and, as far as possible, the right to know and be cared for by his or her parents."

This recognizes a natural right of the offspring to know their biological parents. A court order that prevents this violates the international standard.

**Article 8 — Preservation of Identity**
> "States Parties undertake to respect the right of the child to preserve his or her identity, including nationality, name and family relations as recognized by law without unlawful interference."

Biological parentage is a core element of identity. Alienation that severs the offspring's connection to a biological parent is "unlawful interference" with identity.

**Article 9 — Non-Separation**
> "States Parties shall ensure that a child shall not be separated from his or her parents against their will, except when competent authorities subject to judicial review determine, in accordance with applicable law and procedures, that such separation is necessary for the best interests of the child."

Key elements:
- Separation requires *necessity*, not merely "best interest"
- It must be determined by *competent authorities* with *judicial review*
- One parent's unilateral decision to alienate is not a determination by a competent authority

**Article 10 — Family Reunification**
> "Applications by a child or his or her parents to enter or leave a State Party for the purpose of family reunification shall be dealt with by States Parties in a positive, humane and expeditious manner."

This article implies an affirmative duty to facilitate the parent-offspring bond, not merely to refrain from severing it.

**Article 18 — Both Parents Responsible**
> "States Parties shall use their best efforts to ensure recognition of the principle that both parents have common responsibilities for the upbringing and development of the child."

This directly contradicts custody arrangements that effectively eliminate one parent from the offspring's life.

## European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)

**Article 8 — Right to Respect for Private and Family Life**
> "Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, his home and his correspondence."

The European Court of Human Rights has consistently held that Article 8 protects the parent-child relationship and that state interference must be:
- In accordance with law
- Necessary in a democratic society
- Proportionate to the aim pursued

Alienation violates all three requirements: it is not in accordance with any law, it is not necessary, and severing a biological bond is never proportionate.

**Article 14 — Prohibition of Discrimination**
> "The enjoyment of the rights and freedoms set forth in this Convention shall be secured without discrimination on any ground."

When courts systematically favor one parent over the other (particularly in gendered patterns), it may constitute discrimination in the enjoyment of Article 8 rights.

## Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

**Article 16(3)**
> "The family is the natural and fundamental group unit of society and is entitled to protection by society and the State."

Note the language: "natural and fundamental." The UDHR recognizes that the family is a *natural* unit — not a legal construct created by the state. The state's obligation is to *protect* this natural unit, not to regulate, allocate, or dissolve it.

**Article 12**
> "No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence."

A custody order that severs a biological parent-offspring bond, issued under the broad discretion of "best interest," may constitute "arbitrary interference with family."

## U.S. Constitutional Framework

While the U.S. has not ratified the CRC, U.S. constitutional law provides its own framework for parental rights:

### *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923)
The Fourteenth Amendment liberty interest includes "the right of the individual to... establish a home and bring up children."

### *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925)
"The child is not the mere creature of the State; those who nurture him and direct his destiny have the right, coupled with the high duty, to recognize and prepare him for additional obligations."

### *Stanley v. Illinois*, 405 U.S. 645 (1972)
An unwed father has a constitutionally protected interest in the "companionship, care, custody, and management of his or her children."

### *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)
"The liberty interest at issue in this case — the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children — is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court."

Note: The Supreme Court calls it "the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests" — acknowledging that it predates the Constitution itself. This is, in effect, a recognition of natural rights.

### *Santosky v. Kramer*, 455 U.S. 745 (1982)
Termination of parental rights requires "clear and convincing evidence" — a heightened standard that reflects the fundamental nature of the right.

## The Hierarchy

```
NATURAL LAW
    The parent-offspring bond exists by biology.
    No authority can create or destroy it.
        |
INTERNATIONAL LAW
    CRC, ECHR, UDHR recognize the family as "natural."
    States have a duty to protect, not regulate.
        |
CONSTITUTIONAL LAW (U.S.)
    14th Amendment liberty interest.
    "Oldest of fundamental liberty interests."
        |
FEDERAL LAW
    No comprehensive federal family law.
    (Family law is largely state-level.)
        |
STATE LAW (Illinois)
    750 ILCS 5/ — Marriage and Dissolution Act
    "Best interest of the child" standard
        |
COURT ORDERS
    Custody orders, parenting plans, visitation schedules.
    Operate entirely within the statutory framework.
```

Each level constrains the levels below it. A court order that violates the constitutional right to one's offspring is void. A state law that violates international human rights norms is subject to challenge. And the entire legal framework exists within a natural law context that recognizes the parent-offspring bond as prior to and independent of all law.

## Citing International Frameworks in Illinois Family Court

International law is not binding in Illinois family court, but it is **persuasive authority** — and powerful framing:

### How to Cite
1. **Identify the principle:** "The family is the natural and fundamental group unit of society." (UDHR Art. 16(3))
2. **Connect to U.S. law:** "The Supreme Court has recognized parental rights as 'the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests.'" (*Troxel*)
3. **Show the alignment:** "Both international and domestic authority recognize the parent-offspring bond as natural, fundamental, and prior to state authority."
4. **Challenge the court's standard:** "The 'best interest' standard, as applied in this case, falls below the protection required by international human rights norms and the Supreme Court's recognition of parental rights as fundamental."

### Why This Works
Even though international law is not binding, judges are aware of it. Citing international frameworks:
- Elevates the argument above routine custody disputes
- Creates a record for appellate review
- Invokes authority structures that transcend the trial court's discretion
- Frames the dispute as a human rights issue, not a domestic relations issue
