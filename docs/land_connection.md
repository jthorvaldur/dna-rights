# Land Connection

Biological claim and property claim share the same jurisdictional structure. "Of your flesh" and "of your land" are the same assertion at different scales.

## The Parallel

| Property Claim | Biological Claim |
|---------------|-----------------|
| This is my land | These are my offspring |
| I hold it by natural right | They are of my DNA by nature |
| My title predates the state | My parentage predates any court order |
| The state may recognize my title but did not create it | The state may recognize my parentage but did not create it |
| Allodial title: held absolutely, not from any superior | Natural parentage: exists absolutely, not granted by any authority |
| Encumbrances can be placed on title by legal process | Custody orders can be placed on access by legal process |
| But the land itself remains | But the biological bond itself remains |

## Common Law Foundations

In common law, a man's land was his dominion. His household — including his offspring — was part of that dominion. This was not metaphor; it was the legal structure of the time.

The feudal system organized all of society around land tenure:
- The lord held land from the king
- The lord's domain included everything on the land — including the people
- The lord's offspring were literally "of his domain"

The transition from feudal to individual rights changed *who* holds the bond, but not *that* the bond exists. Individual natural rights replaced the lord's dominion, but the structure is the same: the parent-offspring bond is an inherent part of the parent's natural domain.

## Allodial Title and Natural Parentage

**Allodial title** is absolute ownership of land — ownership that does not derive from any superior lord or government. It is the oldest form of property right, predating feudalism.

**Natural parentage** is the biological parent-offspring bond — a relationship that does not derive from any court order or legal process. It is the oldest form of human relationship, predating any legal system.

The parallel:

- Allodial title cannot be created by government (it exists by right of first possession or natural claim)
- Natural parentage cannot be created by court order (it exists by biological fact)
- Allodial title cannot be extinguished by government (the government did not create it)
- Natural parentage cannot be extinguished by court order (the court did not create it)

In both cases, the legal system may layer its own constructs on top (recorded deeds for land, custody orders for offspring), but the underlying reality is unchanged.

## The Corporate Fiction and Separation

The modern legal system separates the man from his property through layers of abstraction:

**For land:**
- Fee simple title (held from the state, not absolute)
- Property tax (annual rent to the state)
- Eminent domain (state can take the land)
- Zoning (state controls use)
- Liens and encumbrances

**For offspring:**
- Custody orders (state allocates "parenting time")
- Best interest standard (state decides what is best)
- Guardian ad litem (state appoints a representative for the offspring)
- Termination of parental rights (state severs the legal bond)
- Child support enforcement (state controls financial obligation)

In both cases, the corporate legal fiction converts a natural right into a legal privilege that the state can regulate, modify, or revoke. The remedy is the same in both cases: assert the natural right that precedes the legal fiction.

## Practical Jurisdictional Options

Framing custody disputes as property disputes at common law may open different jurisdictional avenues:

### 1. Natural Right of Dominion
Just as a landowner has a natural right to their land that predates recorded title, a biological parent has a natural right to their offspring that predates any custody order.

### 2. Interference with Natural Rights as a Tort
If someone interferes with your land (trespass, nuisance), it is a tort — a civil wrong. If someone interferes with your biological bond to your offspring (alienation, false claims, wrongful separation), the same analysis applies: interference with a natural right.

### 3. Due Process for Natural Property
The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments protect against deprivation of "life, liberty, or property" without due process. If the parent-offspring bond is analyzed as a natural property right (analogous to allodial title), then any court order affecting it must meet the highest standard of due process.

### 4. The Question
When the court treats custody as its jurisdiction, the question becomes: "Does this Court claim the same authority over my offspring that feudal lords claimed over their serfs — that the people belong to the jurisdiction rather than to their natural bonds?"

## Historical Context

In feudal law, offspring were considered part of the lord's domain — they belonged to the land and to the lord. The Enlightenment and the natural rights revolution changed this: people belong to themselves, and the parent-offspring bond belongs to the parent and offspring, not to the state.

Modern family courts have, in practice, reverted to the feudal model: the state asserts jurisdiction over offspring as if they belong to the court's domain rather than to their biological parents. The "best interest of the child" standard gives the court unlimited discretion to override the parent's natural bond — precisely the authority that feudal lords claimed.

The assertion of natural rights in this context is a demand to honor the post-feudal settlement: the parent-offspring bond belongs to the parent and offspring, not to the state.
