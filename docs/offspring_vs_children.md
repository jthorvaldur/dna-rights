# Offspring vs. Children

Legal framing vs. biological framing. The language you accept determines the jurisdiction you submit to.

## "Children" Is a Legal Term

When you say "my children" in a legal proceeding, you are using a term defined by statute. In Illinois:

> **750 ILCS 5/102** — "Child" means a son or daughter of the parties, by birth or adoption.

The word "child" in this context is a legal construct. It describes a relationship that the law defines, the law regulates, and the law can modify. When you accept the term "children," you accept the court's framework:

- The court determines who is a "parent" (legal paternity)
- The court determines the terms of the relationship (custody, visitation)
- The court determines "the best interest of the child" (a standard the court itself defines)
- The court can terminate the legal relationship ("termination of parental rights")

## "Offspring" Is a Biological Term

"Offspring" means the product of biological reproduction. It is not a legal category. It is a fact of nature:

- Offspring are produced by the union of gametes from two biological parents
- The relationship is established at conception and encoded in DNA
- No court proceeding is required to create the relationship
- No court proceeding can alter the biological fact

When you say "my offspring," you assert a relationship the court did not create and cannot adjudicate.

## The Language Shift

Every family court proceeding is conducted in legal language that presupposes the court's jurisdiction:

| Legal Term (Court Jurisdiction) | Biological Term (Natural Right) |
|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Children | Offspring |
| Custody | Natural bond |
| Visitation / Parenting time | Access to offspring |
| Guardian | Parent (biological) |
| Ward | Offspring |
| Best interest of the child | Natural right of the parent-offspring bond |
| Custodial parent | Biological parent with offspring in their care |
| Non-custodial parent | Biological parent denied access to offspring |
| Termination of parental rights | Attempted severance of a biological bond |
| Child support | Parental obligation to offspring (natural duty) |

When you use the left column, you are in the court's jurisdiction. When you use the right column, you assert a jurisdiction the court does not control.

## Why This Matters in Practice

The shift from "children" to "offspring" is not semantic games. It is a jurisdictional challenge:

1. **"Children" implies legal title.** The court adjudicates who has "title" to the children — custody is effectively legal title over a human being. The court decides who holds title and under what conditions.

2. **"Offspring" implies natural fact.** There is no "title" to adjudicate. The biological parent is the biological parent. DNA is not subject to judicial review.

3. **The court cannot adjudicate biology.** A court can determine legal paternity (who the law considers the father). It cannot determine biological parentage (who DNA identifies as the father). These are different questions answered by different authorities.

## Application in Illinois Family Court (750 ILCS 5/)

The Illinois Marriage and Dissolution of Marriage Act uses "child" and "children" throughout. Every use of these terms operates within the statutory framework:

- **750 ILCS 5/602.5** — Allocation of parenting time (uses "child")
- **750 ILCS 5/602.7** — Allocation of significant decision-making (uses "child")
- **750 ILCS 5/603.10** — Restriction of parenting time (uses "child")

When you file a motion or respond to one, and you use the term "offspring" instead of "child," you are making a jurisdictional statement: *This court has jurisdiction over legal relationships. It does not have jurisdiction over biological facts. My offspring are a biological fact.*

This does not guarantee a favorable ruling. But it creates a record. It forces the court to either:
- Acknowledge the distinction (which weakens the court's assumed authority), or
- Ignore the distinction (which can be raised on appeal as a failure to address a jurisdictional challenge)

## The Deeper Point

"Are you claiming the living soul of my DNA?"

The legal system treats human beings — offspring — as subjects of legal process. It assigns "custody" as if assigning property rights. It determines "best interest" as if the state is a better judge of a child's needs than a biological parent.

The offspring distinction cuts through this: these are not legal subjects to be allocated. They are biological offspring of biological parents. The relationship is not created by law and cannot be destroyed by law.
